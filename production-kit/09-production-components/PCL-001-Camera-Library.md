# Purpose

This document defines the standard cinematic camera language used throughout Wild Story Lab productions.

The goal is to ensure consistent visual storytelling across Google Flow, Veo, Imagen, and future AI video generation tools.

---

# Camera Shot Reference

| Camera Shot | Purpose | Best Use |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| Extreme Close-Up | Highlight tiny details and strong emotions | Surprise, tears, smile, object reveal |
| Close-Up | Focus on facial expressions | Dialogue, emotions, reactions |
| Medium Close-Up | Balance face and upper body | Teaching, explaining |
| Medium Shot | Show body language | Character interaction |
| Wide Shot | Display full character and environment | Scene introduction |
| Extreme Wide Shot | Show large environments | World building |

---

# Camera Movement

| Movement | Purpose |
|-----------|----------|
| Static | Calm and stable scenes |
| Dolly In | Build emotion and focus |
| Dolly Out | Reveal surroundings or end a scene |
| Tracking Shot | Follow character movement |
| Orbit Shot | Create dynamic cinematic moments |
| Crane Shot | Epic transitions |
| Overhead Shot | Show scene layout |
| Low Angle | Make character appear confident or heroic |
| High Angle | Make character appear small or vulnerable |
| POV Shot | View from the character's perspective |
| Handheld | Increase energy and urgency |

---

# Camera Selection Guide

## Educational Scene

Recommended

- Medium Shot
- Medium Close-Up
- Close-Up

Avoid

- Handheld
- Fast Orbit

---

## Emotional Scene

Recommended

- Close-Up
- Extreme Close-Up
- Dolly In

---

## Action Scene

Recommended

- Tracking Shot
- Orbit Shot
- Crane Shot

---

## Comedy Scene

Recommended

- Medium Shot
- Wide Shot
- Quick Push In

---

# Best Practices

- Keep camera movement purposeful.
- Avoid excessive motion.
- Prioritize clarity for children.
- Match camera choice to story intent.
- Use smooth transitions whenever possible.
